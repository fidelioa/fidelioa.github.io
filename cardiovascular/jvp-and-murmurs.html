Question 1
A 64-year-old male presents with progressive dyspnea on exertion and episodic lightheadedness over the past 6 months[cite: 1405]. Physical examination reveals a delayed, low-amplitude carotid pulse (pulsus parvus et tardus)[cite: 1406]. On cardiac auscultation, a harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur is heard loudest at the right upper sternal border, radiating to the carotids[cite: 1407]. The second heart sound (S2) is single and paradoxically split[cite: 1408]. Which of the following dynamic auscultatory findings is most expected in this patient? [cite: 1409]
  • A. Softening of the murmur with a handgrip maneuver [cite: 1410]
  • B. Softening of the murmur during the strain phase of the Valsalva maneuver [cite: 1411]
  • C. Intensification of the murmur upon standing from a squatting position [cite: 1412]
  • D. Softening of the murmur following a premature ventricular contraction (PVC) [cite: 1413]
  • E. Decreased intensity of the murmur with passive leg raise [cite: 1414]
Correct Answer: A
Explanation: This presentation is classic for severe aortic stenosis (AS), characterized by the triad of exertional dyspnea, angina, and syncope, along with a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur and pulsus parvus et tardus[cite: 1416]. Handgrip increases afterload (systemic vascular resistance)[cite: 1417]. For AS, an increase in afterload decreases the pressure gradient across the stenotic aortic valve, thereby reducing forward flow and softening the murmur[cite: 1417]. In contrast, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) and mitral regurgitation (MR) behave differently (MR increases with handgrip due to forced backward flow; HCM decreases due to left ventricular expansion)[cite: 1421]. Valsalva strain and standing decrease venous return (preload)[cite: 1422]. Decreasing preload softens AS murmurs but intensifies HCM and mitral valve prolapse (MVP) murmurs[cite: 1423]. Post-PVC, left ventricular filling increases, and contractility is augmented via post-extrasystolic potentiation, increasing forward flow across the aortic valve and intensifying an AS murmur[cite: 1424, 1425].
Educational Objective: Handgrip maneuver increases afterload, which decreases forward flow across the aortic valve, softening the murmur of aortic stenosis[cite: 1426].
Question 2
A 45-year-old female presents with fatigue and peripheral edema[cite: 1428]. On examination, her jugular venous pressure is elevated at 12 cm H2O[cite: 1428]. Inspection of the jugular venous waveform reveals an exceptionally prominent, rapid descent occurring immediately after the second heart sound, while the descent following the "a" wave is blunted[cite: 1429]. A sharp, high-pitched sound is heard early in diastole along the left sternal border[cite: 1430]. Which of the following is the most likely etiology? [cite: 1431]
  • A. Cardiac tamponade [cite: 1432]
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis [cite: 1433]
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation [cite: 1434]
  • D. Tricuspid stenosis [cite: 1435]
  • E. Right ventricular infarction [cite: 1436]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The patient features a prominent, rapid "y" descent (Friedreich's sign) and a high-pitched early diastolic sound (pericardial knock), pathognomonic for constrictive pericarditis[cite: 1438]. In constrictive pericarditis, early diastolic filling is rapid and unimpeded until the rigid, calcified pericardium abruptly halts ventricular expansion, creating a sharp drop and bottoming out of the right atrial pressure (the steep y descent) and producing the pericardial knock[cite: 1439]. Cardiac tamponade features an absent or blunted y descent because the intrapericardial pressure remains high throughout diastole, preventing rapid ventricular filling from the atrium[cite: 1440]. Tricuspid regurgitation presents with a giant, prominent v wave and a sharp y descent, but lacks a pericardial knock and exhibits a prominent x' descent blunting[cite: 1441].
Educational Objective: Constrictive pericarditis presents with a sharp, prominent y descent (Friedreich's sign) and an early diastolic pericardial knock, contrasting with cardiac tamponade which lacks a prominent y descent[cite: 1442].
Question 3
A 28-year-old female presents with an unprovoked episode of palpitations[cite: 1444]. She has a history of mild exertional dyspnea[cite: 1444]. Physical examination reveals a regular cardiac rhythm with a fixed split-second heart sound (S2) across both inspiration and expiration[cite: 1445]. A mid-systolic, crescendo-decrescendo murmur is heard best at the left upper sternal border[cite: 1446]. Which of the following mechanical processes is primarily responsible for the production of this murmur? [cite: 1447]
  • A. Turbulent blood flow across an anatomically stenotic pulmonic valve [cite: 1450]
  • B. Regurgitant flow through an incompetent tricuspid valve [cite: 1451]
  • C. Increased stroke volume and flow across a structurally normal pulmonic valve [cite: 1452]
  • D. Left-to-right shunting of blood directly across an atrial septal defect [cite: 1453]
  • E. Rapid diastolic filling into an oversized right ventricle [cite: 1454]
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: This patient has an atrial septal defect (ASD)[cite: 1456]. The classic presentation of an ASD is a fixed split S2 accompanied by a mid-systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border[cite: 1456]. Crucially, the murmur itself is NOT caused by blood moving across the low-pressure atrial defect, which is functionally silent[cite: 1457]. Instead, the left-to-right shunt creates chronic volume overload of the right atrium and ventricle, leading to an increased stroke volume ejected across a structurally normal pulmonic valve[cite: 1458]. Pulmonic stenosis produces a similar systolic murmur, but it would not feature a fixed split S2; instead, the split would widen normally with inspiration and P2 would be delayed and soft[cite: 1459, 1460].
Educational Objective: The systolic murmur in an ASD is caused by increased blood flow volume across a normal pulmonic valve, not by flow across the septal defect itself[cite: 1461].
Question 4
A 72-year-old male with a history of hypertension and a prior anterior wall myocardial infarction is admitted with acute decompensated heart failure[cite: 1463]. Examination reveals a respiratory rate of 28/min and bilateral pulmonary rales[cite: 1464]. Auscultation at the cardiac apex reveals a soft first heart sound (S1) and a holosystolic murmur that radiates to the left axilla[cite: 1465]. The intensity of this murmur increases when the patient is asked to clench both fists tightly[cite: 1466]. Which of the following changes in left ventricular (LV) dynamics best explains the intensification of this murmur? [cite: 1467]
  • A. Increased LV preload leading to progressive dilation of the mitral annulus [cite: 1468]
  • B. Increased systemic vascular resistance increasing the pressure gradient between the LV and left atrium [cite: 1469]
  • C. Decreased cardiac output reducing the kinetic energy of forward blood flow
  • D. Increased myocardial contractility augmenting the velocity of regurgitant flow
  • E. Reflex bradycardia prolonging the diastolic filling interval
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has ischemic mitral regurgitation (MR), likely secondary to papillary muscle dysfunction or apical remodeling following his prior MI[cite: 1472]. Handgrip exercise increases systemic vascular resistance (SVR), or afterload[cite: 1473]. When afterload increases, the left ventricle faces higher resistance to forward flow through the aortic valve[cite: 1473]. This elevates LV systolic pressure, increasing the pressure gradient between the LV and the lower-pressure left atrium, which forces more blood backward through the incompetent mitral valve, thereby intensifying the murmur[cite: 1474].
Educational Objective: Mitral regurgitation is enhanced by maneuvers that increase afterload (e.g., handgrip) because the increased resistance to forward flow promotes greater regurgitant volume into the left atrium[cite: 1475].
Question 5
A 52-year-old male is evaluated for a 3-week history of worsening dyspnea and orthopnea[cite: 1479]. On examination, his jugular venous pulse displays a highly specific morphology characterized by a highly exaggerated, steep "v" wave and an extraordinarily rapid, deep "y" descent[cite: 1480]. During ventricular systole, a holosystolic murmur is noted along the lower left sternal border[cite: 1481]. The murmur increases significantly in intensity during deep inspiration[cite: 1482]. Which of the following entities is most closely associated with these findings? [cite: 1482]
  • A. Mitral regurgitation [cite: 1483]
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis [cite: 1484]
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation [cite: 1485]
  • D. Right ventricular outflow tract obstruction [cite: 1486]
  • E. Cardiac tamponade [cite: 1487]
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: The patient's JVP waveform demonstrating an exaggerated, prominent v wave and a rapid y descent, combined with a holosystolic murmur at the lower left sternal border that intensifies with inspiration (Carvallo's sign), is highly specific for severe tricuspid regurgitation (TR)[cite: 1489]. During ventricular systole, blood regurgitates backward into the right atrium, adding to the normal venous return and generating a massive "v" (ventricular filling) wave[cite: 1490]. The subsequent rapid ventricular filling in diastole causes a steep "y" descent[cite: 1491].
Educational Objective: Tricuspid regurgitation presents with a prominent v wave on the JVP waveform and a holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border that intensifies with inspiration (Carvallo's sign)[cite: 1492].
Question 6
A 34-year-old male is evaluated during a routine health maintenance exam[cite: 1494]. He is completely asymptomatic and has no significant past medical history[cite: 1495]. Examination of his jugular venous pulsations demonstrates a standard, non-elevated waveform[cite: 1496]. While assessing the patient's neck, the clinician attempts to correlate the JVP waveforms with the cardiac cycle[cite: 1497]. Which of the following physiological events corresponds precisely with the peak of the "a" wave in a healthy individual? [cite: 1498]
  • A. Right ventricular isovolumetric contraction [cite: 1499]
  • B. Right atrial contraction during late diastole [cite: 1500]
  • C. Closure of the tricuspid valve leaflets [cite: 1501]
  • D. Rapid passive filling of the right ventricle [cite: 1502]
  • E. Bulging of the tricuspid valve into the right atrium [cite: 1503]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: In a normal jugular venous pulse waveform, the "a" wave is generated by right atrial contraction (presystole/late diastole)[cite: 1505]. This active contraction forces blood into the right ventricle and simultaneously creates a brief retrograde pressure wave up the superior vena cava and internal jugular vein[cite: 1506]. It occurs just after the P wave on an electrocardiogram (ECG) and immediately precedes the first heart sound (S1)[cite: 1507].
Educational Objective: The JVP "a" wave corresponds to right atrial contraction at the end of ventricular diastole[cite: 1508].
Question 7
A 24-year-old injection drug user presents to the emergency department with a fever of 39.1°C (102.4°F), chills, and pleuritic chest pain[cite: 1512]. Chest radiograph shows multiple nodular, cavitary infiltrates bilaterally[cite: 1513]. On cardiac examination, a high-pitched holosystolic murmur is appreciated at the lower left sternal border[cite: 1513]. Which of the following maneuvers or physiological changes will most likely increase the absolute loudness of this patient's cardiac murmur? [cite: 1514]
  • A. Asking the patient to blow into a syringe (Valsalva strain phase) [cite: 1515]
  • B. Having the patient stand up abruptly from a sitting position [cite: 1516]
  • C. Slow, deep inhalation against no resistance [cite: 1517]
  • D. Performance of a sustained isometric handgrip exercise [cite: 1518]
  • E. Administering amyl nitrite inhalation [cite: 1519]
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: This clinical picture is indicative of right-sided infective endocarditis, specifically involving the tricuspid valve, causing tricuspid regurgitation (TR)[cite: 1521]. Murmurs originating from the right side of the heart (such as TR or pulmonic stenosis) characteristically increase in intensity during inspiration[cite: 1522]. Inspiration reduces intrathoracic pressure, which pulls more venous blood into the right atrium and ventricle, increasing right-sided stroke volume and augmenting the velocity and volume of the regurgitant jet[cite: 1523].
Educational Objective: Right-sided heart murmurs (e.g., tricuspid regurgitation) are characteristically augmented during inspiration due to increased venous return[cite: 1524].
Question 8
A 68-year-old female with a history of systemic sclerosis presents with worsening dyspnea, abdominal distension, and painful pulsation in her neck[cite: 1526]. Her physical exam is notable for a loud pulmonic component of the second heart sound (P2)[cite: 1527]. Her JVP is elevated at 14 cm H2O and reveals massive, single positive waves occurring during ventricular systole that match the carotid pulse[cite: 1528]. Which of the following JVP waveform alterations is expected in this patient? [cite: 1529]
  • A. Blunted or absent "a" wave [cite: 1530]
  • B. Fusion of the "v" wave and "c" wave into a single giant "v" wave [cite: 1531]
  • C. Rapid, hyper-acute "x" descent [cite: 1532]
  • D. Complete absence of the "y" descent [cite: 1533]
  • E. Intermittent, extreme "cannon" waves [cite: 1534]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Systemic sclerosis can cause severe pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), leading to cor pulmonale and functional tricuspid regurgitation (TR) due to right ventricular dilation[cite: 1536]. In severe TR, high-pressure systolic blood regurgitates into the right atrium, obliterating the normal systolic collapse (x descent) and causing the c wave and v wave to fuse into a single, massive giant v wave (sometimes called a systolic wave or ventricularization of the atrial pressure)[cite: 1537].
Educational Objective: Severe tricuspid regurgitation causes loss of the normal systolic x descent and results in a large, fused c-v wave that presents as a prominent systolic venous pulsation in the neck[cite: 1538].
Question 9
A 76-year-old male presents with recurrent syncope during physical activity[cite: 1542]. On examination, a prominent mid-systolic murmur is noted[cite: 1542]. The physician suspects aortic stenosis but wants to differentiate it from hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy (HOCM)[cite: 1543]. The patient is placed in a squatting position, and the murmur softens significantly[cite: 1544]. Which of the following hemodynamic shifts induced by squatting best explains this change if the patient has HOCM? [cite: 1545]
  • A. Decreased systemic afterload and decreased systemic venous return [cite: 1546]
  • B. Increased left ventricular end-diastolic volume (LVEDV) and increased afterload [cite: 1547]
  • C. Increased myocardial contractility and increased heart rate [cite: 1548]
  • D. Decreased afterload leading to reduced left ventricular wall tension [cite: 1549]
  • E. Isolated decrease in preload with stable systemic vascular resistance [cite: 1550]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Squatting simultaneously increases venous return (preload) and systemic vascular resistance (afterload)[cite: 1552]. In HOCM, increasing left ventricular end-diastolic volume (LVEDV) and afterload expands the LV cavity, which separates the interventricular septum from the anterior leaflet of the mitral valve[cite: 1553]. This reduces the dynamic left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) obstruction and softens the HOCM murmur[cite: 1554]. For aortic stenosis, squatting usually increases or maintains murmur intensity due to increased flow across the fixed stenotic valve[cite: 1555].
Educational Objective: Squatting increases both preload and afterload, which increases left ventricular volume and diminishes the dynamic obstruction murmur seen in HOCM[cite: 1556].
Question 10
A 19-year-old college athlete is undergoing a routine physical examination[cite: 1558]. The clinician observes a regular heart rate of 52 beats per minute[cite: 1559]. While examining the patient's neck in the supine position with the head elevated at 45 degrees, the clinician identifies a multi-phasic jugular venous pulsation[cite: 1560]. A soft, low-pitched sound is heard at the apex during early diastole, approximately 0.15 seconds after S2[cite: 1561]. The patient is active and completely asymptomatic[cite: 1562]. Which of the following is the most likely explanation for these clinical findings? [cite: 1562]
  • A. Early-stage restrictive cardiomyopathy [cite: 1563]
  • B. Physiological alterations consistent with a normal, highly trained heart [cite: 1564]
  • C. Occult mitral stenosis with an opening snap [cite: 1565]

  • E. Subclinical tricuspid stenosis with an elevated "a" wave [cite: 1567]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: A low-pitched early diastolic sound heard at the apex in a young, healthy, athletic individual is typically a physiological third heart sound (S3)[cite: 1569]. This sound is caused by rapid, passive ventricular filling into a highly compliant left ventricle[cite: 1570]. A normal, multi-phasic JVP wave at a normal height further confirms the absence of pathology[cite: 1571]. A physiological S3 is common in children, young adults, and trained athletes, and does not signify disease in the absence of other clinical signs or fluid overload[cite: 1572].
Educational Objective: A physiological S3 heart sound is a normal finding in healthy young adults and athletes, representing rapid passive filling into a highly compliant ventricle[cite: 1573].
Question 11
A 61-year-old male presents with episodic lightheadedness and chest discomfort[cite: 1577]. During the physical examination, his heart rate is irregular and varies between 40 and 110 beats per minute[cite: 1578]. While inspecting the internal jugular vein, the clinician notes the intermittent appearance of massive, abrupt, positive venous waves ("cannon a waves") that shoot up toward the jawline[cite: 1579]. Which of the following cardiac conditions is most consistently associated with this physical exam finding? [cite: 1580]
  • A. Persistent Atrial Fibrillation [cite: 1581]
  • B. Complete Atrioventricular (AV) Dissociation [cite: 1582]
  • C. Severe Mitral Regurgitation with Left Atrial Enlargement [cite: 1583]
  • D. Isolated Right Ventricular Hypertrophy [cite: 1584]
  • E. Persistent Sinus Arrythmia [cite: 1585]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Cannon "a" waves occur intermittently when the right atrium contracts against a closed tricuspid valve[cite: 1587]. This occurs during periods of atrioventricular (AV) dissociation, such as complete (third-degree) AV block or ventricular tachycardia, where the atria and ventricles beat independently[cite: 1588]. Whenever a P wave happens to coincide with ventricular systole (QRS complex), the tricuspid valve is closed, and the atrial contraction forces blood backward into the vena cava and jugular system[cite: 1589].
Educational Objective: Intermittent cannon "a" waves indicate AV dissociation, occurring when the atrium contracts against a closed tricuspid valve during ventricular systole[cite: 1590].
Question 12
A 35-year-old immigrant from East Africa presents with progressive exertional dyspnea and orthopnea[cite: 1592]. On examination, a loud, snapping S1 is followed by an opening snap and a low-pitched, rumbling mid-diastolic murmur heard best at the apex in the left lateral decubitus position[cite: 1593]. The clinician notes that the time interval between the second heart sound (S2) and the opening snap (OS) is extremely short[cite: 1594]. This brief S2-OS interval indicates which of the following regarding the patient's condition? [cite: 1595]
  • A. Mild mitral stenosis with low left atrial pressures [cite: 1596]
  • B. Coexisting aortic regurgitation reducing the diastolic filling time [cite: 1597]
  • C. Severe mitral stenosis with significantly elevated left atrial pressure [cite: 1598]
  • D. Excellent left ventricular compliance and normal stroke volume [cite: 1599]
  • E. Calcification of the posterior mitral valve leaflet [cite: 1600]
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: The patient has rheumatic mitral stenosis (MS)[cite: 1602]. The opening snap (OS) occurs when the stenotic mitral valve is forced open by left atrial pressure exceeding left ventricular diastolic pressure[cite: 1602]. In severe mitral stenosis, left atrial pressure (LAP) is profoundly elevated[cite: 1603]. Consequently, at the end of systole, as left ventricular pressure falls, it crosses the highly elevated LAP line much earlier[cite: 1604]. Therefore, a shorter S2-OS interval directly correlates with more severe stenosis and higher atrial pressures[cite: 1605].
Educational Objective: In mitral stenosis, a shorter interval between S2 and the opening snap signifies higher left atrial pressure and greater severity of the valve stenosis[cite: 1606].
Question 13
A 29-year-old male with Marfan syndrome presents with sudden-onset, tearing chest pain radiating to his back[cite: 1610]. While awaiting definitive imaging, his blood pressure is 105/50 mmHg and his heart rate is 110/min[cite: 1611]. On auscultation, a high-pitched, decrescendo diastolic murmur is heard along the right sternal border[cite: 1612]. Which of the following is the most likely physiological cause of these peripheral vascular findings? [cite: 1614]

This widening is caused by an increased stroke volume ejected into the aorta during systole, followed by a rapid, precipitous drop in diastolic pressure due to backflow (runoff) of blood backward into the left ventricle during diastole[cite: 1623].
Educational Objective: Aortic regurgitation produces a widened pulse pressure and hyperdynamic, bounding peripheral signs due to massive systolic stroke volume combined with rapid diastolic runoff[cite: 1624].
Question 14
A 73-year-old female is admitted to the hospital with severe dyspnea, orthopnea, and lower extremity edema[cite: 1626]. On physical exam, her JVP is noted to be markedly elevated at 16 cm H2O[cite: 1627]. The clinician applies firm, steady pressure over the patient's right upper quadrant for 15 seconds while observing the jugular venous column[cite: 1628]. The JVP rises by an additional 4 cm and remains persistently elevated at that new level throughout the entire 15-second compression period[cite: 1629]. This positive hepatojugular reflux test is most diagnostic of which of the following? [cite: 1630]
  • A. Portal hypertension due to intrinsic hepatic cirrhosis [cite: 1631]
  • B. Isolated occlusion of the inferior vena cava above the hepatic veins [cite: 1632]
  • C. Inability of the right ventricle to accommodate increased venous return [cite: 1633]
  • D. Exaggerated respiratory excursions during abdominal compression [cite: 1634]
  • E. Left ventricular failure with preserved right ventricular function [cite: 1635]
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: A positive hepatojugular reflux (HJR) sign is defined as a persistent elevation of the JVP by >3 cm during 10-15 seconds of abdominal compression[cite: 1637]. Abdominal compression manually increases venous return to the right atrium[cite: 1638]. In a healthy heart, the right ventricle adapts to this transient volume load by increasing output without a sustained pressure increase[cite: 1639]. However, in a failing or volume-overloaded right ventricle (or in constrictive/restrictive conditions), the RV is non-compliant and cannot accommodate the increased venous return, causing back-pressure into the jugular system[cite: 1640].
Educational Objective: A positive hepatojugular reflux test indicates right ventricular dysfunction or volume overload, demonstrating that the right heart cannot handle an acute increase in venous preload[cite: 1643].
Question 15
A 38-year-old male presents with a history of fatigue and progressive exertional dyspnea[cite: 1007]. On cardiac examination, a mid-systolic click is heard at the apex, followed immediately by a late systolic crescendo murmur[cite: 1008]. When the patient is asked to perform a Valsalva maneuver (strain phase), how will the timing of the click and the duration of the murmur change? [cite: 1009]
  • A. The click occurs later in systole, and the murmur becomes shorter in duration [cite: 1010]
  • B. The click occurs earlier in systole, and the murmur becomes longer in duration [cite: 1010]
  • C. The click occurs earlier in systole, and the murmur becomes shorter in duration [cite: 1010]
  • D. The click occurs later in systole, and the murmur becomes longer in duration [cite: 1010]
  • E. Neither the timing of the click nor the murmur duration undergoes any shift [cite: 1010]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has mitral valve prolapse (MVP)[cite: 1010]. The strain phase of the Valsalva maneuver decreases venous return, which reduces left ventricular end-diastolic volume (making the LV cavity smaller)[cite: 1026]. With a smaller LV volume, the redundant mitral valve leaflets prolapse into the left atrium earlier in systole[cite: 1027]. Consequently, the mid-systolic click shifts closer to S1 (occurs earlier), and because the valve is incompetent for a longer portion of systole, the murmur begins earlier and becomes longer in duration[cite: 1028].
Educational Objective: Decreasing left ventricular volume (e.g., via Valsalva strain or standing) causes mitral valve prolapse to occur earlier in systole, shifting the click closer to S1 and lengthening the murmur[cite: 1029].
Question 16

Auscultation reveals a low-pitched, rumbling diastolic murmur at the apex that mimics mitral stenosis[cite: 1656]. [cite: 1659]

  • B. Positional variability of the murmur and absence of a true opening snap [cite: 1045]

Educational Objective: Left atrial myxoma can mimic mitral stenosis by obstructing the mitral orifice, but it is characterized by positional variability of the murmur and the absence of a true valvular opening snap[cite: 1048].
Question 17
A 49-year-old female with severe COPD is evaluated for worsening peripheral edema and ascites[cite: 1049]. On physical examination, her jugular venous pressure is significantly elevated[cite: 1050]. During inspiration, instead of the expected decline, her jugular venous pressure paradoxically rises by 3 cm[cite: 1051]. Which of the following mechanical states is directly responsible for this specific physical sign? [cite: 1052]
  • A. Severe collapse of the superior vena cava during intrathoracic pressure drops [cite: 1053]
  • B. Inelasticity of the pericardium or right ventricular myocardium restricting filling [cite: 1679]
  • C. Acute reversal of blood flow through a patent ductus arteriosus [cite: 1680]
  • D. Severe backward failure of the left ventricle forcing fluid into pulmonary capillaries
  • E. Exaggerated compliance of the right atrial wall [cite: 1681]
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: A paradoxical rise in JVP during inspiration is known as Kussmaul's sign[cite: 1065]. Normally, inspiration decreases intrathoracic pressure, which increases venous return into the right side of the heart, causing the JVP column to drop[cite: 1066]. However, when the right ventricle or pericardium is highly non-compliant (as in constrictive pericarditis, restrictive cardiomyopathy, or severe isolated right heart failure), the right heart cannot accept the standard inspiratory surge in venous volume[cite: 1067]. As a result, the incoming blood backs up into the internal jugular veins, causing the JVP to rise paradoxically[cite: 1068].
Educational Objective: Kussmaul's sign is a paradoxical rise in JVP during inspiration, caused by a rigid pericardium or non-compliant right ventricle that prevents the heart from absorbing the normal inspiratory increase in venous return[cite: 1069].
