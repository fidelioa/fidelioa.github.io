A 64-year-old male presents with progressive dyspnea on exertion and episodic lightheadedness over the past 6 months. Physical examination reveals a delayed, low-amplitude carotid pulse (pulsus parvus et tardus). On cardiac auscultation, a harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur is heard loudest at the right upper sternal border, radiating to the carotids. The second heart sound (S2) is single and paradoxically split. Which of the following dynamic auscultatory findings is most expected in this patient?
  • A. Softening of the murmur with a handgrip maneuver
  • B. Softening of the murmur during the strain phase of the Valsalva maneuver
  • C. Intensification of the murmur upon standing from a squatting position
  • D. Softening of the murmur following a premature ventricular contraction (PVC)
  • E. Decreased intensity of the murmur with passive leg raise
Correct Answer: A
Explanation: This presentation is classic for severe aortic stenosis (AS), characterized by the triad of exertional dyspnea, angina, and syncope, along with a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur and pulsus parvus et tardus. Handgrip increases afterload (systemic vascular resistance). For AS, an increase in afterload decreases the pressure gradient across the stenotic aortic valve, thereby reducing forward flow and softening the murmur. In contrast, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) and mitral regurgitation (MR) behave differently (MR increases with handgrip due to forced backward flow; HCM decreases due to left ventricular expansion). Valsalva strain and standing decrease venous return (preload). Decreasing preload softens AS murmurs but intensifies HCM and mitral valve prolapse (MVP) murmurs. Post-PVC, left ventricular filling increases, and contractility is augmented via post-extrasystolic potentiation, increasing forward flow across the aortic valve and intensifying an AS murmur.
Educational Objective: Handgrip maneuver increases afterload, which decreases forward flow across the aortic valve, softening the murmur of aortic stenosis.
A 45-year-old female presents with fatigue and peripheral edema. On examination, her jugular venous pressure is elevated at 12 cm H2O. Inspection of the jugular venous waveform reveals an exceptionally prominent, rapid descent occurring immediately after the second heart sound, while the descent following the "a" wave is blunted. A sharp, high-pitched sound is heard early in diastole along the left sternal border. Which of the following is the most likely etiology?
  • A. Cardiac tamponade
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation
  • D. Tricuspid stenosis
  • E. Right ventricular infarction
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The patient features a prominent, rapid "y" descent (Friedreich's sign) and a high-pitched early diastolic sound (pericardial knock), pathognomonic for constrictive pericarditis. In constrictive pericarditis, early diastolic filling is rapid and unimpeded until the rigid, calcified pericardium abruptly halts ventricular expansion, creating a sharp drop and bottoming out of the right atrial pressure (the steep y descent) and producing the pericardial knock. Cardiac tamponade features an absent or blunted y descent because the intrapericardial pressure remains high throughout diastole, preventing rapid ventricular filling from the atrium. Tricuspid regurgitation presents with a giant, prominent v wave and a sharp y descent, but lacks a pericardial knock and exhibits a prominent x' descent blunting.
Educational Objective: Constrictive pericarditis presents with a sharp, prominent y descent (Friedreich's sign) and an early diastolic pericardial knock, contrasting with cardiac tamponade which lacks a prominent y descent.
A 28-year-old female presents with an unprovoked episode of palpitations. She has a history of mild exertional dyspnea. Physical examination reveals a regular cardiac rhythm with a fixed split-second heart sound (S2) across both inspiration and expiration. A mid-systolic, crescendo-decrescendo murmur is heard best at the left upper sternal border. Which of the following mechanical processes is primarily responsible for the production of this murmur?
  • A. Turbulent blood flow across an anatomically stenotic pulmonic valve
  • B. Regurgitant flow through an incompetent tricuspid valve
  • C. Increased stroke volume and flow across a structurally normal pulmonic valve
  • D. Left-to-right shunting of blood directly across an atrial septal defect
  • E. Rapid diastolic filling into an oversized right ventricle
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: This patient has an atrial septal defect (ASD). The classic presentation of an ASD is a fixed split S2 accompanied by a mid-systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border. Crucially, the murmur itself is NOT caused by blood moving across the low-pressure atrial defect, which is functionally silent. Instead, the left-to-right shunt creates chronic volume overload of the right atrium and ventricle, leading to an increased stroke volume ejected across a structurally normal pulmonic valve. Pulmonic stenosis produces a similar systolic murmur, but it would not feature a fixed split S2; instead, the split would widen normally with inspiration and P2 would be delayed and soft.
Educational Objective: The systolic murmur in an ASD is caused by increased blood flow volume across a normal pulmonic valve, not by flow across the septal defect itself.
A 72-year-old male with a history of hypertension and a prior anterior wall myocardial infarction is admitted with acute decompensated heart failure. Examination reveals a respiratory rate of 28/min and bilateral pulmonary rales. Auscultation at the cardiac apex reveals a soft first heart sound (S1) and a holosystolic murmur that radiates to the left axilla. The intensity of this murmur increases when the patient is asked to clench both fists tightly. Which of the following changes in left ventricular (LV) dynamics best explains the intensification of this murmur?
  • A. Increased LV preload leading to progressive dilation of the mitral annulus
  • B. Increased systemic vascular resistance increasing the pressure gradient between the LV and left atrium
  • C. Decreased cardiac output reducing the kinetic energy of forward blood flow
  • D. Increased myocardial contractility augmenting the velocity of regurgitant flow
  • E. Reflex bradycardia prolonging the diastolic filling interval
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has ischemic mitral regurgitation (MR), likely secondary to papillary muscle dysfunction or apical remodeling following his prior MI. Handgrip exercise increases systemic vascular resistance (SVR), or afterload. When afterload increases, the left ventricle faces higher resistance to forward flow through the aortic valve. This elevates LV systolic pressure, increasing the pressure gradient between the LV and the lower-pressure left atrium, which forces more blood backward through the incompetent mitral valve, thereby intensifying the murmur.
Educational Objective: Mitral regurgitation is enhanced by maneuvers that increase afterload (e.g., handgrip) because the increased resistance to forward flow promotes greater regurgitant volume into the left atrium.
A 52-year-old male is evaluated for a 3-week history of worsening dyspnea and orthopnea. On examination, his jugular venous pulse displays a highly specific morphology characterized by a highly exaggerated, steep "v" wave and an extraordinarily rapid, deep "y" descent. During ventricular systole, a holosystolic murmur is noted along the lower left sternal border. The murmur increases significantly in intensity during deep inspiration. Which of the following entities is most closely associated with these findings?
  • A. Mitral regurgitation
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation
  • D. Right ventricular outflow tract obstruction
  • E. Cardiac tamponade
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: The patient's JVP waveform demonstrating an exaggerated, prominent v wave and a rapid y descent, combined with a holosystolic murmur at the lower left sternal border that intensifies with inspiration (Carvallo's sign), is highly specific for severe tricuspid regurgitation (TR). During ventricular systole, blood regurgitates backward into the right atrium, adding to the normal venous return and generating a massive "v" (ventricular filling) wave. The subsequent rapid ventricular filling in diastole causes a steep "y" descent.
Educational Objective: Tricuspid regurgitation presents with a prominent v wave on the JVP waveform and a holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border that intensifies with inspiration (Carvallo's sign).
A 34-year-old male is evaluated during a routine health maintenance exam. He is completely asymptomatic and has no significant past medical history. Examination of his jugular venous pulsations demonstrates a standard, non-elevated waveform. While assessing the patient's neck, the clinician attempts to correlate the JVP waveforms with the cardiac cycle. Which of the following physiological events corresponds precisely with the peak of the "a" wave in a healthy individual?
  • A. Right ventricular isovolumetric contraction
  • B. Right atrial contraction during late diastole
  • C. Closure of the tricuspid valve leaflets
  • D. Rapid passive filling of the right ventricle
  • E. Bulging of the tricuspid valve into the right atrium
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: In a normal jugular venous pulse waveform, the "a" wave is generated by right atrial contraction (presystole/late diastole). This active contraction forces blood into the right ventricle and simultaneously creates a brief retrograde pressure wave up the superior vena cava and internal jugular vein. It occurs just after the P wave on an electrocardiogram (ECG) and immediately precedes the first heart sound (S1).
Educational Objective: The JVP "a" wave corresponds to right atrial contraction at the end of ventricular diastole.
A 24-year-old injection drug user presents to the emergency department with a fever of 39.1°C (102.4°F), chills, and pleuritic chest pain. Chest radiograph shows multiple nodular, cavitary infiltrates bilaterally. On cardiac examination, a high-pitched holosystolic murmur is appreciated at the lower left sternal border. Which of the following maneuvers or physiological changes will most likely increase the absolute loudness of this patient's cardiac murmur?
  • A. Asking the patient to blow into a syringe (Valsalva strain phase)
  • B. Having the patient stand up abruptly from a sitting position
  • C. Slow, deep inhalation against no resistance
  • D. Performance of a sustained isometric handgrip exercise
  • E. Administering amyl nitrite inhalation
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: This clinical picture is indicative of right-sided infective endocarditis, specifically involving the tricuspid valve, causing tricuspid regurgitation (TR). Murmurs originating from the right side of the heart (such as TR or pulmonic stenosis) characteristically increase in intensity during inspiration. Inspiration reduces intrathoracic pressure, which pulls more venous blood into the right atrium and ventricle, increasing right-sided stroke volume and augmenting the velocity and volume of the regurgitant jet.
Educational Objective: Right-sided heart murmurs (e.g., tricuspid regurgitation) are characteristically augmented during inspiration due to increased venous return.
A 68-year-old female with a history of systemic sclerosis presents with worsening dyspnea, abdominal distension, and painful pulsation in her neck. Her physical exam is notable for a loud pulmonic component of the second heart sound (P2). Her JVP is elevated at 14 cm H2O and reveals massive, single positive waves occurring during ventricular systole that match the carotid pulse. Which of the following JVP waveform alterations is expected in this patient?
  • A. Blunted or absent "a" wave
  • B. Fusion of the "v" wave and "c" wave into a single giant "v" wave
  • C. Rapid, hyper-acute "x" descent
  • D. Complete absence of the "y" descent
  • E. Intermittent, extreme "cannon" waves
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Systemic sclerosis can cause severe pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), leading to cor pulmonale and functional tricuspid regurgitation (TR) due to right ventricular dilation. In severe TR, high-pressure systolic blood regurgitates into the right atrium, obliterating the normal systolic collapse (x descent) and causing the c wave and v wave to fuse into a single, massive giant v wave (sometimes called a systolic wave or ventricularization of the atrial pressure).
Educational Objective: Severe tricuspid regurgitation causes loss of the normal systolic x descent and results in a large, fused c-v wave that presents as a prominent systolic venous pulsation in the neck.
A 76-year-old male presents with recurrent syncope during physical activity. On examination, a prominent mid-systolic murmur is noted. The physician suspects aortic stenosis but wants to differentiate it from hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy (HOCM). The patient is placed in a squatting position, and the murmur softens significantly. Which of the following hemodynamic shifts induced by squatting best explains this change if the patient has HOCM?
  • A. Decreased systemic afterload and decreased systemic venous return
  • B. Increased left ventricular end-diastolic volume (LVEDV) and increased afterload
  • C. Increased myocardial contractility and increased heart rate
  • D. Decreased afterload leading to reduced left ventricular wall tension
  • E. Isolated decrease in preload with stable systemic vascular resistance
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Squatting simultaneously increases venous return (preload) and systemic vascular resistance (afterload). In HOCM, increasing left ventricular end-diastolic volume (LVEDV) and afterload expands the LV cavity, which separates the interventricular septum from the anterior leaflet of the mitral valve. This reduces the dynamic left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) obstruction and softens the HOCM murmur. For aortic stenosis, squatting usually increases or maintains murmur intensity due to increased flow across the fixed stenotic valve.
Educational Objective: Squatting increases both preload and afterload, which increases left ventricular volume and diminishes the dynamic obstruction murmur seen in HOCM.
A 19-year-old college athlete is undergoing a routine physical examination. The clinician observes a regular heart rate of 52 beats per minute. While examining the patient's neck in the supine position with the head elevated at 45 degrees, the clinician identifies a multi-phasic jugular venous pulsation. A soft, low-pitched sound is heard at the apex during early diastole, approximately 0.15 seconds after S2. The patient is active and completely asymptomatic. Which of the following is the most likely explanation for these clinical findings?
  • A. Early-stage restrictive cardiomyopathy
  • B. Physiological alterations consistent with a normal, highly trained heart
  • C. Occult mitral stenosis with an opening snap
  • D. Constrictive pericarditis with a soft pericardial knock
  • E. Subclinical tricuspid stenosis with an elevated "a" wave
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: A low-pitched early diastolic sound heard at the apex in a young, healthy, athletic individual is typically a physiological third heart sound (S3). This sound is caused by rapid, passive ventricular filling into a highly compliant left ventricle. A normal, multi-phasic JVP wave at a normal height further confirms the absence of pathology. A physiological S3 is common in children, young adults, and trained athletes, and does not signify disease in the absence of other clinical signs or fluid overload.
Educational Objective: A physiological S3 heart sound is a normal finding in healthy young adults and athletes, representing rapid passive filling into a highly compliant ventricle.
A 61-year-old male presents with episodic lightheadedness and chest discomfort. During the physical examination, his heart rate is irregular and varies between 40 and 110 beats per minute. While inspecting the internal jugular vein, the clinician notes the intermittent appearance of massive, abrupt, positive venous waves ("cannon a waves") that shoot up toward the jawline. Which of the following cardiac conditions is most consistently associated with this physical exam finding?
  • A. Persistent Atrial Fibrillation
  • B. Complete Atrioventricular (AV) Dissociation
  • C. Severe Mitral Regurgitation with Left Atrial Enlargement
  • D. Isolated Right Ventricular Hypertrophy
  • E. Persistent Sinus Arrythmia
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Cannon "a" waves occur intermittently when the right atrium contracts against a closed tricuspid valve. This occurs during periods of atrioventricular (AV) dissociation, such as complete (third-degree) AV block or ventricular tachycardia, where the atria and ventricles beat independently. Whenever a P wave happens to coincide with ventricular systole (QRS complex), the tricuspid valve is closed, and the atrial contraction forces blood backward into the vena cava and jugular system.
Educational Objective: Intermittent cannon "a" waves indicate AV dissociation, occurring when the atrium contracts against a closed tricuspid valve during ventricular systole.
A 35-year-old immigrant from East Africa presents with progressive exertional dyspnea and orthopnea. On examination, a loud, snapping S1 is followed by an opening snap and a low-pitched, rumbling mid-diastodial murmur heard best at the apex in the left lateral decubitus position. The clinician notes that the time interval between the second heart sound (S2) and the opening snap (OS) is extremely short. This brief S2-OS interval indicates which of the following regarding the patient's condition?
  • A. Mild mitral stenosis with low left atrial pressures
  • B. Coexisting aortic regurgitation reducing the diastolic filling time
  • C. Severe mitral stenosis with significantly elevated left atrial pressure
  • D. Excellent left ventricular compliance and normal stroke volume
  • E. Calcification of the posterior mitral valve leaflet
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: The patient has rheumatic mitral stenosis (MS). The opening snap (OS) occurs when the stenotic mitral valve is forced open by left atrial pressure exceeding left ventricular diastolic pressure. In severe mitral stenosis, left atrial pressure (LAP) is profoundly elevated. Consequently, at the end of systole, as left ventricular pressure falls, it crosses the highly elevated LAP line much earlier. Therefore, a shorter S2-OS interval directly correlates with more severe stenosis and higher atrial pressures.
Educational Objective: In mitral stenosis, a shorter interval between S2 and the opening snap signifies higher left atrial pressure and greater severity of the valve stenosis.

While awaiting definitive imaging, his blood pressure is 105/50 mmHg and his heart rate is 110/min. On auscultation, a high-pitched, decrescendo diastolic murmur is heard along the right sternal border. Which of the following is the most likely physiological cause of these peripheral vascular findings?
  • A. Severely reduced stroke volume with reflex systemic vasoconstriction
  • B. Rapid runoff of blood into both the left ventricle and a widened systemic compliance network
  • C. Marked elevation in right ventricular end-diastolic pressure
  • D. Severe isolated systolic hypertension due to aortic stiffness
  • E. Systemic arteriovenous shunting within the upper extremities
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient with Marfan syndrome has developed an acute Type A aortic dissection that has retrograded into the aortic root, causing acute severe aortic regurgitation (AR). Bounding peripheral pulses (Water-hammer pulse) and capillary pulsations (Quincke's sign) reflect a widened pulse pressure. This widening is caused by an increased stroke volume ejected into the aorta during systole, followed by a rapid, precipitous drop in diastolic pressure due to backflow (runoff) of blood backward into the left ventricle during diastole.
Educational Objective: Aortic regurgitation produces a widened pulse pressure and hyperdynamic, bounding peripheral signs due to massive systolic stroke volume combined with rapid diastolic runoff.
A 73-year-old female is admitted to the hospital with severe dyspnea, orthopnea, and lower extremity edema. On physical exam, her JVP is noted to be markedly elevated at 16 cm H2O. The clinician applies firm, steady pressure over the patient's right upper quadrant for 15 seconds while observing the jugular venous column. The JVP rises by an additional 4 cm and remains persistently elevated at that new level throughout the entire 15-second compression period. This positive hepatojugular reflux test is most diagnostic of which of the following?
  • A. Portal hypertension due to intrinsic hepatic cirrhosis
  • B. Isolated occlusion of the inferior vena cava above the hepatic veins
  • C. Inability of the right ventricle to accommodate increased venous return
  • D. Exaggerated respiratory excursions during abdominal compression
  • E. Left ventricular failure with preserved right ventricular function
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: A positive hepatojugular reflux (HJR) sign is defined as a persistent elevation of the JVP by >3 cm during 10-15 seconds of abdominal compression. Abdominal compression manually increases venous return to the right atrium. In a healthy heart, the right ventricle adapts to this transient volume load by increasing output without a sustained pressure increase. However, in a failing or volume-overloaded right ventricle (or in constrictive/restrictive conditions), the RV is non-compliant and cannot accommodate the increased venous return, causing back-pressure into the jugular system.
Educational Objective: A positive hepatojugular reflux test indicates right ventricular dysfunction or volume overload, demonstrating that the right heart cannot handle an acute increase in venous preload.
A 38-year-old male presents with a history of fatigue and progressive exertional dyspnea. On cardiac examination, a mid-systolic click is heard at the apex, followed immediately by a late systolic crescendo murmur. When the patient is asked to perform a Valsalva maneuver (strain phase), how will the timing of the click and the duration of the murmur change?
  • A. The click occurs later in systole, and the murmur becomes shorter in duration
  • B. The click occurs earlier in systole, and the murmur becomes longer in duration
  • C. The click occurs earlier in systole, and the murmur becomes shorter in duration
  • D. The click occurs later in systole, and the murmur becomes longer in duration
  • E. Neither the timing of the click nor the murmur duration undergoes any shift
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has mitral valve prolapse (MVP). The strain phase of the Valsalva maneuver decreases venous return, which reduces left ventricular end-diastolic volume (making the LV cavity smaller). With a smaller LV volume, the redundant mitral valve leaflets prolapse into the left atrium earlier in systole. Consequently, the mid-systolic click shifts closer to S1 (occurs earlier), and because the valve is incompetent for a longer portion of systole, the murmur begins earlier and becomes longer in duration.
Educational Objective: Decreasing left ventricular volume (e.g., via Valsalva strain or standing) causes mitral valve prolapse to occur earlier in systole, shifting the click closer to S1 and lengthening the murmur.
