A 64-year-old male presents with progressive dyspnea on exertion and episodic lightheadedness over the past 6 months. Physical examination reveals a delayed, low-amplitude carotid pulse (pulsus parvus et tardus). On cardiac auscultation, a harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur is heard loudest at the right upper sternal border, radiating to the carotids. The second heart sound (S2) is single and paradoxically split. Which of the following dynamic auscultatory findings is most expected in this patient?
  • A. Softening of the murmur with a handgrip maneuver
  • B. Softening of the murmur during the strain phase of the Valsalva maneuver
  • C. Intensification of the murmur upon standing from a squatting position
  • D. Softening of the murmur following a premature ventricular contraction (PVC)
  • E. Decreased intensity of the murmur with passive leg raise
Correct Answer: A
Explanation: This presentation is classic for severe aortic stenosis (AS), characterized by the triad of exertional dyspnea, angina, and syncope, along with a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur and pulsus parvus et tardus. Handgrip increases afterload (systemic vascular resistance). For AS, an increase in afterload decreases the pressure gradient across the stenotic aortic valve, thereby reducing forward flow and softening the murmur. In contrast, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) and mitral regurgitation (MR) behave differently.
Educational Objective: Handgrip maneuver increases afterload, which decreases forward flow across the aortic valve, softening the murmur of aortic stenosis.
A 45-year-old female presents with fatigue and peripheral edema. On examination, her jugular venous pressure is elevated at 12 cm H2O. Inspection of the jugular venous waveform reveals an exceptionally prominent, rapid descent occurring immediately after the second heart sound, while the descent following the "a" wave is blunted. A sharp, high-pitched sound is heard early in diastole along the left sternal border. Which of the following is the most likely etiology?
  • A. Cardiac tamponade
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation
  • D. Tricuspid stenosis
  • E. Right ventricular infarction
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The patient features a prominent, rapid "y" descent (Friedreich's sign) and a high-pitched early diastolic sound (pericardial knock), pathognomonic for constrictive pericarditis. In constrictive pericarditis, early diastolic filling is rapid and unimpeded until the rigid, calcified pericardium abruptly halts ventricular expansion, creating a sharp drop and bottoming out of the right atrial pressure.
Educational Objective: Constrictive pericarditis presents with a sharp, prominent y descent (Friedreich's sign) and an early diastolic pericardial knock.
A 28-year-old female presents with an unprovoked episode of palpitations. She has a history of mild exertional dyspnea. Physical examination reveals a regular cardiac rhythm with a fixed split-second heart sound (S2) across both inspiration and expiration. A mid-systolic, crescendo-decrescendo murmur is heard best at the left upper sternal border. Which of the following mechanical processes is primarily responsible for the production of this murmur?
  • A. Turbulent blood flow across an anatomically stenotic pulmonic valve
  • B. Regurgitant flow through an incompetent tricuspid valve
  • C. Increased stroke volume and flow across a structurally normal pulmonic valve
  • D. Left-to-right shunting of blood directly across an atrial septal defect
  • E. Rapid diastolic filling into an oversized right ventricle
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: This patient has an atrial septal defect (ASD). The classic presentation of an ASD is a fixed split S2 accompanied by a mid-systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border. Crucially, the murmur itself is NOT caused by blood moving across the low-pressure atrial defect, which is functionally silent. Instead, the left-to-right shunt creates chronic volume overload of the right atrium and ventricle, leading to an increased stroke volume ejected across a structurally normal pulmonic valve.
Educational Objective: The systolic murmur in an ASD is caused by increased blood flow volume across a normal pulmonic valve, not by flow across the septal defect itself.
A 72-year-old male with a history of hypertension and a prior anterior wall myocardial infarction is admitted with acute decompensated heart failure. Examination reveals a respiratory rate of 28/min and bilateral pulmonary rales. Auscultation at the cardiac apex reveals a soft first heart sound (S1) and a holosystolic murmur that radiates to the left axilla. The intensity of this murmur increases when the patient is asked to clench both fists tightly. Which of the following changes in left ventricular (LV) dynamics best explains the intensification of this murmur?
  • A. Increased LV preload leading to progressive dilation of the mitral annulus
  • B. Increased systemic vascular resistance increasing the pressure gradient between the LV and left atrium
  • C. Decreased cardiac output reducing the kinetic energy of forward blood flow
  • D. Increased myocardial contractility augmenting the velocity of regurgitant flow
  • E. Reflex bradycardia prolonging the diastolic filling interval
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has ischemic mitral regurgitation (MR). Handgrip exercise increases systemic vascular resistance (SVR), or afterload. When afterload increases, the left ventricle faces higher resistance to forward flow through the aortic valve. This elevates LV systolic pressure, increasing the pressure gradient between the LV and the lower-pressure left atrium, which forces more blood backward through the incompetent mitral valve.
Educational Objective: Mitral regurgitation is enhanced by maneuvers that increase afterload (e.g., handgrip) because the increased resistance to forward flow promotes greater regurgitant volume into the left atrium.
A 52-year-old male is evaluated for a 3-week history of worsening dyspnea and orthopnea. On examination, his jugular venous pulse displays a highly specific morphology characterized by a highly exaggerated, steep "v" wave and an extraordinarily rapid, deep "y" descent. During ventricular systole, a holosystolic murmur is noted along the lower left sternal border. The murmur increases significantly in intensity during deep inspiration. Which of the following entities is most closely associated with these findings?
  • A. Mitral regurgitation
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation
  • D. Right ventricular outflow tract obstruction
  • E. Cardiac tamponade
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: The patient's JVP waveform demonstrating an exaggerated, prominent v wave and a rapid y descent, combined with a holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border that intensifies with inspiration (Carvallo's sign), is highly specific for severe tricuspid regurgitation (TR). During ventricular systole, blood regurgitates backward into the right atrium, generating a massive "v" wave.
Educational Objective: Tricuspid regurgitation presents with a prominent v wave on the JVP waveform and a holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border that intensifies with inspiration (Carvallo's sign).
