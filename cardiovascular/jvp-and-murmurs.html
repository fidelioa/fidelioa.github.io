A 64-year-old male presents with progressive dyspnea on exertion and episodic lightheadedness over the past 6 months. Physical examination reveals a delayed, low-amplitude carotid pulse (pulsus parvus et tardus). On cardiac auscultation, a harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur is heard loudest at the right upper sternal border, radiating to the carotids. The second heart sound (S2) is single and paradoxically split. Which of the following dynamic auscultatory findings is most expected in this patient?
  • A. Softening of the murmur with a handgrip maneuver
  • B. Softening of the murmur during the strain phase of the Valsalva maneuver
  • C. Intensification of the murmur upon standing from a squatting position
  • D. Softening of the murmur following a premature ventricular contraction (PVC)
  • E. Decreased intensity of the murmur with passive leg raise
Correct Answer: A
Explanation: This presentation is classic for severe aortic stenosis (AS), characterized by the triad of exertional dyspnea, angina, and syncope, along with a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur and pulsus parvus et tardus. Handgrip increases afterload (systemic vascular resistance). For AS, an increase in afterload decreases the pressure gradient across the stenotic aortic valve, thereby reducing forward flow and softening the murmur. In contrast, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) and mitral regurgitation (MR) behave differently (MR increases with handgrip due to forced backward flow; HCM decreases due to left ventricular expansion). Valsalva strain and standing decrease venous return (preload). Decreasing preload softens AS murmurs but intensifies HCM and mitral valve prolapse (MVP) murmurs. Post-PVC, left ventricular filling increases, and contractility is augmented via post-extrasystolic potentiation, increasing forward flow across the aortic valve and intensifying an AS murmur.
Educational Objective: Handgrip maneuver increases afterload, which decreases forward flow across the aortic valve, softening the murmur of aortic stenosis.
A 45-year-old female presents with fatigue and peripheral edema. On examination, her jugular venous pressure is elevated at 12 cm H2O. Inspection of the jugular venous waveform reveals an exceptionally prominent, rapid descent occurring immediately after the second heart sound, while the descent following the "a" wave is blunted. A sharp, high-pitched sound is heard early in diastole along the left sternal border. Which of the following is the most likely etiology?
  • A. Cardiac tamponade
  • B. Constrictive pericarditis
  • C. Severe tricuspid regurgitation
  • D. Tricuspid stenosis
  • E. Right ventricular infarction
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The patient features a prominent, rapid "y" descent (Friedreich's sign) and a high-pitched early diastolic sound (pericardial knock), pathognomonic for constrictive pericarditis. In constrictive pericarditis, early diastolic filling is rapid and unimpeded until the rigid, calcified pericardium abruptly halts ventricular expansion, creating a sharp drop and bottoming out of the right atrial pressure (the steep y descent) and producing the pericardial knock. Cardiac tamponade features an absent or blunted y descent because the intrapericardial pressure remains high throughout diastole, preventing rapid ventricular filling from the atrium. Tricuspid regurgitation presents with a giant, prominent v wave and a sharp y descent, but lacks a pericardial knock and exhibits a prominent x' descent blunting.
Educational Objective: Constrictive pericarditis presents with a sharp, prominent y descent (Friedreich's sign) and an early diastolic pericardial knock, contrasting with cardiac tamponade which lacks a prominent y descent.
