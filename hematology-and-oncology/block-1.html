A 32-year-old female presents with progressive fatigue and a crawling sensation in her legs at night. Lab evaluation demonstrates a hemoglobin level of 9.2 g/dL, mean corpuscular volume (MCV) of 71 fL, and a high red cell distribution width (RDW). Which of the following biochemical profiles is most consistent with this patient's underlying condition?
  • A. Low serum iron, high ferritin, low TIBC
  • B. Low serum iron, low ferritin, high TIBC
  • C. High serum iron, high ferritin, low TIBC
  • D. Low serum iron, high ferritin, high TIBC
  • E. Normal serum iron, normal ferritin, normal TIBC
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient presents with iron deficiency anemia (IDA), evidenced by microcytic anemia (MCV < 80 fL), an elevated RDW, and restless legs syndrome. In IDA, total body iron stores are exhausted, leading to low serum ferritin (the most specific marker). The liver compensates for low iron availability by increasing the production of transferrin, which directly increases the Total Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC). Consequently, transferrin saturation falls dramatically.
Educational Objective: Iron deficiency anemia is characterized by low serum iron, low ferritin, elevated TIBC, and decreased transferrin saturation.
A 64-year-old male presents with deep dull back pain and constipation. Laboratory testing shows a serum calcium of 11.2 mg/dL, a serum creatinine of 2.1 mg/dL, and mild normocytic anemia. A peripheral blood smear demonstrates red blood cells stacked like coins. Which of the following structural or cellular changes is directly responsible for this specific blood smear finding?
  • A. Extramedullary hematopoiesis within the splenic red pulp
  • B. Loss of negative surface charge on erythrocytes due to excess serum paraproteins
  • C. Mechanical destruction of erythrocytes by calcified marrow spicules
  • D. Impaired alpha-globin chain synthesis leading to target cells
  • E. Autoimmune IgG coating of red blood cells causing spherocytosis
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The clinical triad of hypercalcemia, renal insufficiency, anemia, and bone pain (CRAB) indicates Multiple Myeloma. The stacking of red blood cells on the peripheral smear is known as Rouleaux formation. Monoclonal plasma cells secrete excess immunoglobulins (M-protein/paraproteins). Educational Objective: Rouleaux formation in multiple myeloma is driven by elevated serum paraproteins masking the normal negative surface charge of red blood cells.

What is the underlying enzymatic mechanism defective in this patient?
  • A. Inability to generate NADPH, leaving glutathione in an oxidized state
  • B. Impaired glycolysis due to pyruvate kinase failure
  • C. Defective anchor proteins for decay-accelerating factor (DAF)
  • D. Alteration of spectrin and ankyrin cross-linking structures
  • E. Deficient heme attachment to beta-globin chains
Correct Answer: A
Explanation: This patient has Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency triggered by an oxidative drug (sulfa medication). G6PD is the rate-limiting enzyme of the pentose phosphate pathway, which generates NADPH. Educational Objective: G6PD deficiency impairs NADPH production, preventing the reduction of glutathione and leaving erythrocytes vulnerable to oxidative stress and hemolysis.

What morphological feature would be most prominent on his peripheral blood film?
  • A. Spherocytes
  • B. Target cells
  • C. Schistocytes
  • D. Sickle cells
  • E. Echinocytes
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: This patient is experiencing intravascular hemolysis secondary to a mechanical heart valve, a form of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA) or macrovascular shearing. She is started on continuous unfractionated heparin, but her partial thromboplastin time (PTT) fails to prolong appropriately, remaining close to baseline despite escalations in dose. What inherited thrombophilia fits this profile?
  • A. Factor V Leiden mutation
  • B. Prothrombin G20210A mutation
  • C. Antithrombin III deficiency
  • D. Protein C deficiency
  • E. Antiphospholipid syndrome
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: Unfractionated heparin works by binding to and exponentially augmenting the activity of antithrombin III (ATIII), which subsequently neutralizes thrombin (Factor IIa), Factor Xa, and other clotting enzymes. Educational Objective: Antithrombin III deficiency causes apparent resistance to heparin therapy because heparin requires ATIII to exert its anticoagulant action.

A 69-year-old female is started on standard therapeutic warfarin for new-onset atrial fibrillation. Two days later, she develops severe, well-demarcated, painful violaceous lesions that progress rapidly to hemorrhagic bullae and necrosis over her breasts and thighs. What underlying inherited cofactor deficiency predisposed this patient to this complication?
  • A. Factor V Leiden mutation
  • B. Antithrombin III deficiency
  • C. Protein C or S deficiency
  • D. Dysfibrinogenemia
  • E. Factor XI deficiency
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: Warfarin inhibits Vitamin K Epoxide Reductase, blocking the synthesis of clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, as well as endogenous anticoagulants Protein C and Protein S. Protein C has a very short half-life (~6 hours) compared to structural procoagulants like Factor II (prothrombin). In patients with an underlying, inherited Protein C or S deficiency, this drop causes microvascular thrombosis within cutaneous capillaries, leading to Warfarin-Induced Skin Necrosis.
Educational Objective: Warfarin-induced skin necrosis occurs in patients with underlying Protein C or S deficiencies due to a rapid drop in anticoagulant factors relative to procoagulant factors early in treatment.
