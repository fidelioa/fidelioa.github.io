A 24-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of a dry cough, mild dyspnea, and painful red nodules on her shins. Chest radiography demonstrates prominent bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy. Laboratory testing reveals elevated serum calcium and elevated serum angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) levels. Biopsy of a lymph node is most likely to show which of the following histological features?
  • A. Necrotizing granulomas with central caseous debris
  • B. Non-caseating granulomas containing multinucleated giant cells
  • C. Sheets of atypical squamous epithelial cells forming keratin pearls
  • D. Intra-alveolar deposits of lipid-rich PAS-positive proteinaceous material
  • E. Extensive interstitial deposition of dense collagen and reticulin fibers
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient presents with systemic sarcoidosis, supported by the triad of respiratory symptoms, erythema nodosum (painful nodules on shins), and bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy. Sarcoidosis is a systemic disease characterized by hypercalcemia (due to 1-alpha-hydroxylase expression by activated macrophages converting vitamin D) and elevated ACE levels. The histopathological hallmark of sarcoidosis is non-caseating (non-necrotizing) granulomas containing epithelioid histiocytes and Langhans multinucleated giant cells, differentiating it from tuberculosis, which features caseating granulomas.
Educational Objective: Sarcoidosis is characterized by non-caseating granulomas in affected tissues, classically presenting with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, hypercalcemia, and erythema nodosum.
A 62-year-old male with a 45 pack-year smoking history presents with a chronic cough, progressive exertional dyspnea, and a barrel chest. Pulmonary function testing shows an FEV1/FVC ratio of 0.52 and a significantly decreased diffusing capacity of the lung for carbon monoxide (DLCO). What is the primary pathogenetic mechanism directly driving the structural alveolar destruction in this patient's disease?
  • A. Autoimmune-mediated destruction of Type II pneumocytes
  • B. Excess elastase activity overriding the inhibitory capacity of alpha-1 antitrypsin
  • C. Hyperplasia of bronchial submucosal glands from mechanical stress
  • D. Smooth muscle hypertrophy and eosinophilic infiltration of terminal bronchioles
  • E. Intra-alveolar fluid accumulation inhibiting surfactant functionality
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has emphysema, a form of Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) caused by tobacco smoking. Cigarette smoke recruits neutrophils and macrophages to the alveoli, which release proteases and elastase. Simultaneously, oxidants in smoke inactivate local alpha-1 antitrypsin (an anti-elastase protector). This protease-antiprotease imbalance causes unchecked destruction of the elastic tissue walls of the alveoli, decreasing surface area for gas exchange (manifested as a reduced DLCO) and causing airflow limitation (low FEV1/FVC).
Educational Objective: Centrilobular emphysema in smokers is caused by a protease-antiprotease imbalance, where excess elastase destroys alveolar elastic walls.
A 54-year-old female undergoes an elective hysterectomy. On postoperative day 3, she develops sudden-onset pleuritic chest pain and acute dyspnea. Her oxygen saturation drops to 86% on room air. Computed tomography pulmonary angiography confirms a saddle pulmonary embolism. Which of the following changes in arterial blood gas parameters is most expected prior to intervention?
  • A. Respiratory acidosis with an elevated PaCO2
  • B. Metabolic acidosis with a normal anion gap
  • C. Respiratory alkalosis with a decreased PaCO2
  • D. Combined respiratory and metabolic acidosis
  • E. Normal pH with an elevated PaO2
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: An acute pulmonary embolism creates a ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) mismatch (alveoli are ventilated but not perfused due to the clot), leading to hypoxia. Hypoxia triggers the peripheral chemoreceptors to drive hyperventilation. Increased minute ventilation clears carbon dioxide efficiently, resulting in a decreased arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2) and an elevated blood pH, which is diagnostic of an acute respiratory alkalosis.
Educational Objective: Acute pulmonary embolism characteristically induces hyperventilation secondary to hypoxia, resulting in a respiratory alkalosis with hypocapnia (low PaCO2).

  • A. Montelukast
  • B. Zileuton
  • C. Zafirlukast
  • D. Albuterol
  • E. Fluticasone

Explanation: The leukotriene pathway is a key contributor to bronchoconstriction, mucosal edema, and inflammatory cell infiltration in asthma. An infant born at 27 weeks gestation develops severe grunting, nasal flaring, and intercostal retractions immediately after birth. He is diagnosed with Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (NRDS). What is the primary chemical composition of the substance missing in this infant's alveoli, and which cells are responsible for its synthesis?
  • A. Sphingomyelin; Type I pneumocytes
  • B. Dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine; Type II pneumocytes
  • C. Mucin glycoproteins; Goblet cells
  • D. Phosphatidylglycerol; Club cells
  • E. Alpha-surfactant protein; Endothelial cells
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Premature infants often lack adequate pulmonary surfactant, leading to NRDS. Pulmonary surfactant reduces alveolar surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse during expiration (atelectasis). Surfactant is synthesized and secreted by **Type II pneumocytes** (which also act as stem cells to regenerate alveolar epithelium). The primary active lipid component of surfactant is **dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC)**, also called lecithin. Surfactant production increases significantly after 35 weeks of gestation.
Educational Objective: NRDS is caused by a deficiency of pulmonary surfactant (primarily dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine), which is synthesized by Type II pneumocytes.

  • A. Increased left atrial pressure causing retrograde hydrostatic fluid transudation
  • B. Endothelial injury and increased alveolar-capillary permeability leading to protein-rich exudate
  • C. Obstruction of central lymphatic drainage by a mediastinal mass
  • D. Loss of alveolar surfactant due to aspiration of gastric acid
  • E. Chronic fibrotic replacement of the basement membrane scaffold
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: This patient has Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) secondary to sepsis. ARDS criteria include acute onset, bilateral opacities not fully explained by volume overload, and severe hypoxemia (PaO2/FiO2 ratio ≤ 300 mmHg). Educational Objective: ARDS is characterized by non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema driven by neutrophil-mediated endothelial damage and increased alveolar-capillary permeability.

What physiologic mechanism explains this acute hypoventilation after oxygen administration?
  • A. Activation of central chemoreceptors by high oxygen tension
  • B. Loss of the hypoxic respiratory drive due to blunting of peripheral chemoreceptors
  • C. Decreased dead space ventilation via generalized pulmonary vasodilation
  • D. Leftward shift of the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve
  • E. Direct toxic inhibition of diaphragmatic contraction
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Patients with severe, chronic COPD often retain carbon dioxide, leading to blunted central chemoreceptors that normally respond to changes in pH and PaCO2.   • B. Total blood oxygen content
  • C. Partial pressure of oxygen dissolved in arterial plasma (PaO2)
  • D. P50 value of the oxygen-hemoglobin curve
  • E. Skin coloration balance
Correct Answer: C
Explanation: Carbon monoxide (CO) binds to hemoglobin with an affinity over 200 times greater than oxygen, forming carboxyhemoglobin.
